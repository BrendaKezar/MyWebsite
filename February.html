My odd hobby: Certificates

February 2024

Done GIFfrom Done GIFs

I am so glad January is over. It hasn't even been that bad weather-wise, but I am over it. We only had one negative temp cold snap, and it hasn't been very snowy. It's been unusually warm (by North Dakota standards), foggy, and drizzly. Usually, I LIKE foggy and drizzly . . . but I don't like ice. And that's what we've been dealing with constantly.

Not only have we been breaking records for warm temperatures and even having our first ever December flood warning, there are other indications that things are really, really out-of-whack. Normally, winter is quiet. One of the things that always startles me the first few days of warm weather is how noisy it is when all the birds come back. But this year, I think a lot of them have hung around. I had cars behind me so I couldn't get a picture, but as I drove through the parking lot near the Gorecki Center on campus, I looked up, and two of the trees were full of robins.

ROBINS!

Robins . . . in North Dakota . . . in January! It's unnerving. And what are they even eating??? Robins are carnivores. I know they will eat berries and stuff if they arrive back in spring too early for the bugs to be out, but it can't sustain them long term. So what are these winter robins doing?

Come to think of it . . . when we had our one cold snap so far this winter, hubby found two dead birds near our back pine, about a week apart.In spite of the fact that I always say, "How the heck do birds survive a North Dakota winter," we actually don't find too many dead birds around the house in winter, usually.

Hubby shrugged it off, saying maybe they were already sickly, but now I wonder: maybe they were also birds that don't belong here right now. Maybe, like the robins, they were lured into hanging around due to the warmer temps, and that one sudden cold snap got them.

The other reason I'm glad January is in the rear-view mirror is because we lost Roscoe this month. My grumpy old man finally left his earthly bonds. We knew it was coming, but that didn't make it any less heartbreaking.

His body has always been a source of misery for him, and it was only getting worse. His allergies went from needing seasonal medication to needing year-round medication, and even then, he was still frequently miserably itchy. He scratched his eyebrow off TWICE last summer.

He has also been on antacids his whole life, because in spite of his desire to eat ALL THE THINGS, he suffered from a sensitive tummy. We cycled through dozens and dozens of different dog foods, with no relief for him. Periodically, his digestive tract would start making noises like a drain trying to back up, and he would stop eating and shiver with pain. We called it having "squirtle belly." This really sucked because eating was his second favorite hobby next to camping.

And, as he got older, he started getting these weird tumors all over. They were diagnosed as benign, but they grew back if removed. And he seemed to be suffering from osteoarthritis as he got older, as well. That's why we had him on the monthly monoclonal antibody shots, to help relieve that. My poor old guy had been taking a handful of pills every day for the better part of his life, and for the last five or sixth months, he was having to visit the vet monthly. He wasn't very happy about it, either.

I'm really glad that this last camping season, he and I spent so much time going off-trail. It's was his favorite thing in the world. So I can take some comfort in knowing that his last camping season was a good one.

As you've noticed, I'm back to working on my html on off-site pages. Part of that is because of my class this semester (more on that later), but part of it is because the free site I had been using for a little while ticked me off.

Out of the blue, I received an email saying the free host had disabled my site and put it up for deletion since it wasn't being used. Sure, I hadn't put up any new pages for a bit and had just been doing my updates on Wordpress, but I hadn't realized that if you don't log in and update your site every now and then with the free host, they will disable and delete!

Granted, it's my own fault for not paying attention to all the terms and rules when I signed up. And, it is a FREE site, so I can't complain too much. But between the "dang, I don't know if I can update the site every month via html" and the other limitations with the free host (no videos), I decided maybe I would try a paid host again. Paid hosts don't give a fig if I update or not as long as I send them money every month, lol.

As it turned out, I was able to get a good deal with my prior paid host. So I went back and re-subscribed with them for a year's service. It's actually good because it encourages me to actually use the hosting and practice my html. After all, I don't like paying for something I'm not using, so I feel OBLIGATED to use it!

Speaking of classes . . .

This semester at the University of North Dakota, I’m taking a class for the info systems minor: web development.

The class gives two different options for writing your code: Bluegriffon and Visual Studio Code. I had never heard of Bluegriffon before this class, but it looks like it is a lot like SeaMonkey, which I dabbled with in the past.

BlueGriffon (and SeaMonkey) are WYSIWYG editors, much like the "website builder" apps offered by many webhosts. Frankly, I've never cared for them.

And if I'm being perfectly honest, WYSIWYGs feel a little like cheating, when it comes to learning HTML. Heck, when I started doing HTML again, even IDEs like VSC felt like cheating, lol! Back in my day, we wrote our code in Notepad and liked it (uphill, both ways, in the snow, ha-ha).

But when I starteed programming again, there was a lot of stuff that made me make Clint Eastwood grumpy-old-man face.

Ughh Annoyed GIFfrom Ughh GIFs

Back in "my day," we programmed using a text editor like Notepad, and we compiled our programs on $1K compiler (a huge expense and source of stress for a student trying to learn the profession.)

And now you have IDEs that are FREE that auto-populate your code for you. A missing closing tag was one of the most common--and hard to find--errors back in my day.

And so many of the languages today are interpretive. Even for those that aren't, there are other options. For the $1K compiler of my "old days," that I was so excited about back then when I was able to grab one on sale for $300, I now have today's on my computer for free.

So my dive back into programming has very much felt like the whole "kids have it so easy these days" thing, lol. And being allowed to work on WYSIWYG feels like that. But I'm sure that people felt the same way about calculators in school, too.

But you know what? Really, all of this SHOULD be easier than it was "back in my day." Isn't that the whole purpose of progress and innovation? So that we no longer have to walk five miles to the river to get water, but the water should come to us?

And maybe that's it. Maybe "cold-coding" is a dying art. We went from my original programming days (boot disk), to coding with compiling, to IDEs, to WYSIWYGs. I often say that memorizing dates and facts, a staple of education "back in my day," is a dumb standard that should be dead. Why should I memorize things that I can just Google? Why cold-program something like html when I can just whip it up with a WYSIWYG? Then I can spend my time worrying about learning Python, Java, etc., instead. And, although there aren't WYSIWYGs for those languages, I can use IDEs to program them much faster than "back in my day."

I am not going top rail against these easier methods. I am going to embrace the improvements that help me be more efficient while still requiring me to develop some skills. I feel like, for me personally, IDEs are a useful tool, while WYSIWYG's don't allow me to develop my programming muscles. So I will continue to develop my pages through an IDE (as this page was created), but I will continue to avoid WYSIWYGs like Blue Griffon, Sea Monkey, etc., when I can. I'm going to rock it old school with skittles and hot pockets, while using an IDE that's going to make sure I close my tags and have semicolons where they are expected (which, now that I look at it, means IDEs are sort of like Grammar checkers like Grammarly; they don't write your code for you, they just check it for accuracy.)

(Except, of course, when I'm lazy and pressed for time with this author page; then I will happily skip the programming and use the (basically) WYSIWYG editor that is Wordpress, lol.)

Meanwhile, work continues on the short stories. Two are going well, if slowly, but the first one (the original one I was so enthused about) has really stalled. The problem is, I don't have an ending for it. I posted before how I wasn't sure where the story was going to end. So the story and the characters just keep going on and on with no ending, no resolution to their story, in sight.

The story is ridiculously long and will have to have major cutting during the revison process, but I don't want to start cutting stuff out until I know what the ending is going to be. So I'm kind of at an impasse with this story until I come up with an endpoint.

Circling back to the writer's conference, one of the late additions to the author line up is Sterling HolyWhiteMountain. Something in his conference bio caught my attention: "He is an unrecognized citizen of the Blackfeet Nation.".

So, of course, I had to Google him and find out what THAT was all about. Here are some links to interviews/essays/etc.

A few days later, I learned about this recent controversy in the fashion industry.

Kendra Scott collaboration with Wrangler draws fire over charges of cultural appropriation

Kendra Scott faces cultural appropriation accusations over collection

Quotes from the articles:

However, critics quickly took to social media to call out the brands for appropriating Native American culture, specifically criticizing the turquoise jewelry designs that have, for hundreds of years, been a significant part of Navajo, Hopi, Zuni, Pueblo and other tribes’ cultural and spiritual practices.

“This is not just knockoff turquoise, but it’s a knockoff of a culture. Turquoise has been prized in the Americas for centuries, and particularly turquoise works that are wearable,”...“We wear the turquoise because it was given to us long ago, and with it there are songs. There are prayers."

I had never before thought about the possibility that turquoise should be off limits due to its sacred nature to some cultures. After all, I grew up in the seventies, when turquoise jewelry was all the rage. I'm pretty sure I still have some of my old 70s turquoise jewelry in a box somewhere (not to mention the black hills gold and turquoise ring I just bought a year a two ago!)

And up until a few years ago, I thought blood quantum was actually a good (if questionable) idea. On the surface, it seemed the point was to make sure that things that belonged/benefitted indigeneous people were reserved for indigeneous people. For example, I thought it made it easier(?) to enforce the Native Arts and Crafts Act, which attempts to protect Native artisans by prohibiting non-indigenous-created goods from being marketed and sold with any label/wording that may misrepresent the items as being indigeneous-made.

Now I know it's a complex, complicated issue that is often abused and even used by some in power to "un-Indian" tribal political rivals, etc. And the idea was originally created by the colonizers as yet another way to eventually get rid of indigeneous people altogether. In other words, it's always been a weapon!

These are all incredibly complex issues. Along with varying support for blood quantum, the very definition of cultural appropriation varies among ethnic groups/nationalities/cultures/tribes, and even among individuals within those groups.

A good example is the school mascot situation. When my university was debating whether to discontinue their mascot, they polled the tribes of North Dakota to get their opinion (and disclaimer: I'm not sure if they polled ALL the tribes, or just what they consider the "major tribes"). Four out of five of the polled tribes in North Dakota voted that it was offensive and should be removed. One of the polled tribes voted that it was honoring their people and should be kept.

So I'm going to continue listening to and learning from those willing to teach and share their views. And I will continue to try to navigate these complex issues as respectfully as possible.
